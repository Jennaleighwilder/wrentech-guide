Module 1

What a website actually is

Before you build anything, you need to know what you're actually building. Here it is, no jargon:

A website has 3 parts

1. The front (the part people SEE)
Pictures. Words. Buttons. Forms. The pretty stuff. Tech people call this the "frontend."

2. The back (the part that DOES things)
When someone fills out a form, something has to actually receive that info and save it. That invisible machinery is the "backend."

3. The address (where it LIVES)
Your website needs to live somewhere on the internet (called hosting) and have an address people can type in (called a domain, like yourname.com).

Think of it like a restaurant. Frontend is the dining room (what customers see). Backend is the kitchen (where the actual work happens). Hosting is the building itself. Domain is the street address.

What a website is supposed to DO for your business

A website is not a digital business card. It's a 24/7 employee that does ONE of these jobs:

📞 Get them to call/book

Best for: service businesses. Therapists, contractors, photographers, healers, coaches.

Goal: Calendar booking or phone call

💌 Get their email

Best for: people not ready to buy yet. Writers, course creators, consultants.

Goal: Email signup → nurture → sale later

🛒 Get them to buy NOW

Best for: products, digital downloads, low-priced services.

Goal: Click → checkout → done

If your website tries to do all 3 at once, it will do none of them well. Pick ONE main goal. Everything on the page should push toward that goal. We'll come back to this in Module 2.

Module 2

Plan your ONE goal

Before you touch a single tool, answer these questions. Out loud. Or write them down. Whatever works for your brain.

The 5 questions

  1. Who is this for? One specific person, not "everyone."
  2. What problem do you solve for them? In one sentence.
  3. What do you want them to DO when they land on your site? One action.
  4. What would make them trust you in 10 seconds? Photos? Reviews? Credentials?
  5. What's stopping them from doing it? Price? Fear? They don't know you?

Example: "I'm Cindy. I help anxious women in their 40s breathe through panic attacks. When someone lands on my site, I want them to book a free 15-minute call. They'll trust me because I have 200+ testimonials. They're scared it'll be expensive, so I need to lead with the FREE call."

The 3-second test

When someone lands on your homepage, they should know within 3 seconds:

  • What you do
  • Who it's for
  • What to click next

If your homepage says "Welcome to my site!" you've already lost. Welcome is what your door says. Your homepage needs to be a sales pitch, not a greeting.

Write your "one-liner" right now

Fill in the blanks. Out loud. Then write it down:

"I help [who] do [what] so they can [result]."

Examples:

  • "I help burnt-out moms create 30-minute home workouts so they can feel strong without leaving the house."
  • "I help small business owners with their bookkeeping so they can stop dreading tax season."
  • "I help indie authors design book covers that sell so they can compete with traditional publishers."

This one-liner becomes the headline at the top of your homepage. It tells visitors in 5 seconds whether you're for them. Without it, they leave.

Module 4

Set up your free accounts (the boring but critical part)

You need a few free accounts before you can do anything. Make these now. It'll take 20 minutes total. Get them all done in one sitting so you don't have to come back.

These accounts are what hold your website, your form data, your email list, and your domain. Without them, you're building on sand. The good news: every one of these has a real free tier you can stay on forever.

The 5 accounts you need (in order)

  1. Email address you'll actually check

    Use an email you check daily. Not your personal "myname1987@aol.com" you haven't logged into since 2015. Make a new free Gmail if you need one: gmail.com

    Tip: Use something like yourname.work@gmail.com or yourbusinessname@gmail.com. You can upgrade to a custom email (you@yourbusiness.com) later.

  2. GitHub (free) — where your code lives

    GitHub is like Google Drive but for code. Even if you don't write code yourself, your website's code will live here. It's free forever for what you need.

    1. Go to github.com/signup
    2. Use the email from step 1
    3. Pick a username. This is public. Use something professional. Examples: jenna-west, floraflowerfarm. Avoid numbers if you can.
    4. Verify the email they send you
    5. Skip every "upgrade to Pro" screen. The free tier is fine.

    What's GitHub for? Two things. (1) It saves every version of your site, so if you break something you can roll back. (2) It connects to your hosting (Vercel/Netlify) so when you save a change, your live website updates automatically.

  3. Vercel OR Netlify (free) — where your site is hosted

    This is where your website actually lives on the internet. Both are free for personal/small business use. Pick ONE. Don't make both.

    Vercel

    FREE. Forever. For most small sites.

    Pick this if you'll use Next.js, React, or any modern AI builder (v0, Bolt, Lovable all deploy here). Sign in with your GitHub account — no separate password.

    vercel.com/signup →

    Netlify

    FREE. Forever. For most small sites.

    Pick this if you're building a simple HTML site or using Carrd-style tools. Has built-in form handling (no extra service needed). Sign in with GitHub too.

    netlify.com/signup →

    Do NOT sign up for Vercel and Netlify. Pick one. Your future self will thank you. If you don't know which, pick Vercel — it works with more AI tools.

  4. MailerLite (free) — your email list

    When people give you their email, it has to go somewhere. MailerLite holds those emails and lets you send newsletters. The free plan covers up to 1,000 subscribers and 12,000 emails per month. That's more than enough for your first year.

    1. Go to mailerlite.com
    2. Click "Sign up free"
    3. They'll ask you what you do. Be honest — if you have a business idea, type it. They approve most accounts within 24 hours.

    Why MailerLite and not Mailchimp? Mailchimp's free plan is now junk (only 500 contacts, limited features). MailerLite's free plan is actually usable. It also has a built-in landing page builder if you want a backup option.

  5. Cal.com (free) — your booking calendar

    If you want people to book appointments with you, you need this. Cal.com is the open-source version of Calendly. Free for individual use, forever.

    1. Go to cal.com/signup
    2. Sign up with Google (easiest) or email
    3. Pick your username. This becomes your booking URL: cal.com/yourusername
    4. Connect your Google Calendar / Apple Calendar / Outlook so it knows when you're busy
    5. Set your default event (15-min call, 30-min consult, etc.)

    Why Cal.com over Calendly? Calendly's free plan only allows ONE event type. Cal.com lets you have multiple (free call, paid session, follow-up) on the free plan. Also Cal.com is open source, which means it's not going to suddenly paywall everything in 2 years.

Checklist: am I done?

  • I have a working email I check daily
  • I have a GitHub account (free)
  • I have either Vercel OR Netlify (not both)
  • I have MailerLite (free)
  • I have Cal.com (free)
  • I wrote down all 5 passwords somewhere safe (a password manager, a notebook, anywhere except a sticky note on your monitor)

About passwords: Write them down. In a notebook. Or use a free password manager like Bitwarden. If you lose access to one of these accounts, you can lose access to your live website. This isn't dramatic — it happens weekly.

Module 5

Build the actual site

This is the part that scares people. Don't let it. Whichever path you picked, the steps below get you to a live website you can show people.

Building with Carrd (recommended for Path A)

  1. Go to carrd.co and click "Start"

    You'll be asked to sign up. Free account is fine. Use the same email as before.

  2. Pick a template

    Browse the "Profile" or "Landing" categories. Pick one that's close to what you want — you'll change the words and colors anyway. Don't agonize. Pick in 5 minutes.

  3. Replace every word with YOUR words

    Click any text to edit it. Use your one-liner from Module 2 as the headline. Don't write "Welcome." Write what you DO.

  4. Replace the image

    Use a real photo of you or your work if possible. People trust faces. If you don't have one, use a free photo from Unsplash or Pexels — both are 100% free for any use, no attribution required.

  5. Set up your ONE button

    Make sure your main button does the thing you decided in Module 2:

    • Book a call: Link the button to your Cal.com URL (cal.com/yourusername)
    • Get their email: Carrd has a built-in form. Connect it to MailerLite in Carrd's settings.
    • Buy now: Use Stripe Payment Links (covered in Module 9)

  6. Hit "Publish"

    Free Carrd gives you a URL like yourname.carrd.co. That's your live website. Test it on your phone.

  7. You're done with the build. Now go to Module 6.

Building with Claude (recommended for Path B)

  1. Open Claude.ai (free)

    Sign in with the email from Module 4.

  2. Start a new chat and paste this prompt template

    Build me a single-page website as ONE HTML file with CSS and JavaScript all included.

About my business:
- I help: [WHO YOUR CUSTOMERS ARE]
- I do: [WHAT YOU DO]
- The main goal of this page is: [BOOK A CALL / GET AN EMAIL / SELL A PRODUCT]
- My booking link is: [YOUR CAL.COM LINK or write SKIP]
- My email signup will go to: MailerLite (give me the embed instructions)

Design preferences:
- Style: [WARM AND FRIENDLY / SLEEK AND MODERN / BOLD AND COLORFUL]
- Colors: [PICK 2-3 COLORS YOU LIKE]
- Include: a hero with my one-liner, 3 reasons to work with me, a section of testimonials (use placeholders I'll replace), and a clear call-to-action button at the bottom.

Make it mobile-friendly. Make it load fast. No frameworks, just plain HTML/CSS/JS in one file I can save as index.html.

    Fill in the brackets with YOUR info. Then paste it in Claude. Hit send.

  3. Claude will write the code

    It'll appear in a code block or an "Artifact" panel on the right. You can preview it right there.

  4. Ask for changes in plain English

    Examples:

    • "Make the headline bigger"
    • "Change the green to a warmer orange"
    • "Move the testimonials above the button"
    • "Add a section about my pricing"

  5. When it's right, download the HTML file

    Click the download or copy button. Save the file as index.html on your desktop.

  6. Deploy it to Vercel (your hosting)

    Three options, easiest first:

    • Easiest: Drag-and-drop. Go to vercel.com/new → drag your folder containing index.html. Done.
    • Better long-term: Upload it to GitHub first, then connect to Vercel. We walk through this in Module 15.

    Vercel gives you a URL like your-site-abc123.vercel.app. That's your live site. Test on your phone.

Some people use v0.dev, Bolt, or Lovable instead. Same idea: describe your site, get code, deploy. We recommend Claude first because it's the most patient with long, detailed requests and the free tier is the most usable.

Building with Cursor (recommended for Path C)

This path takes longer the first time but you'll actually understand what's happening.

  1. Download and install Cursor

    Go to cursor.com, download for your operating system (Mac, Windows, Linux). Install it like any other app.

  2. Open Cursor and create a new project folder

    File → Open Folder → make a new folder on your desktop called something like my-website. Open it.

  3. Open the chat (Cmd+L on Mac, Ctrl+L on Windows)

    Type: "Create a single-page website for [my business]. Make index.html, style.css, and script.js. The goal of the page is [your goal]."

    Cursor will create the files for you. You can watch it happen.

  4. Preview your site

    The simplest way: right-click on index.html in the file list, choose "Reveal in Finder/Explorer," double-click the file. It opens in your browser. That's your site, running locally.

  5. Edit by chatting

    Highlight any chunk of code, hit Cmd/Ctrl+K, type what you want changed. Or chat in the side panel: "make the hero section taller and add a background gradient."

  6. When ready, push to GitHub then deploy

    Cursor has built-in Git (the system that pushes code to GitHub). The first time is hardest. See Module 15 for the full walkthrough.

Whatever path you took, at this point you should have a live website URL. Open it on your phone. Send it to a friend. Look at it on a tablet. If it's there and it loads, you've done what 80% of small business owners never finish. Now we make it actually work.

Module 6

Make your buttons actually DO something

This is where most people get stuck. They build a beautiful site... and then the "Contact Me" button leads to a broken form. Here's how to make every button work.

Every button on your site does one of 3 things: goes to another link (like your calendar), sends you a message (a form), or takes payment. We'll handle all 3.

Type 1: A button that LINKS to something (easiest)

These are buttons that send people to another page or app:

  • "Book a call" → links to your Cal.com URL
  • "Follow me on Instagram" → links to your Instagram
  • "Read the menu" → links to a PDF or page
  • "Buy on Etsy" → links to your Etsy shop

How: In your builder or code, wrap your button in an <a> tag (link tag). In Carrd, click the button and paste the URL. In Claude/Cursor, just tell it "make this button link to https://cal.com/me".

Type 2: A button that SENDS YOU A MESSAGE (a contact form)

This is the trickiest part for beginners. When someone fills out a form, that info has to GO SOMEWHERE. Most beginners build the form and forget to connect it. The form looks fine but nobody can actually contact them.

Test your form yourself before showing the world. Submit a test message. Make sure it arrives in your email. Do it on a phone too.

Walkthrough: connecting a form with Formspree

  1. Sign up at formspree.io with your email

  2. Click "New Form"

    Give it a name like "Contact Form"

  3. Copy your form's endpoint URL

    It looks like https://formspree.io/f/abc123

  4. Paste it in your form's "action" attribute

    If you're using Claude or Cursor, tell the AI: "Change my contact form to submit to https://formspree.io/f/abc123 using POST."

    In Carrd: click the form, go to "After submit", choose "Submit form data" and paste the URL.

  5. Submit a test message

    Fill out the form on your live site. Check your email. The first time, Formspree will send you a verification email — click the link inside.

  6. You're done. Every form submission now hits your inbox.

Module 7

Add a booking calendar

If your business runs on appointments, calls, or sessions — this is the most important section in the whole guide. A calendar button removes 90% of the back-and-forth.

Set up Cal.com from scratch

  1. Sign up at cal.com/signup

    Free forever for individuals. Use the same email you've been using.

  2. Pick your booking URL

    You'll choose a username. Your booking page will be cal.com/yourusername. Pick something professional. cal.com/jenna-west or cal.com/flossys-flowers.

  3. Connect your real calendar

    Cal.com needs to see when you're busy. Connect Google Calendar (if you use Gmail), Apple Calendar (iCloud), or Outlook. This is critical — without this, you'll get double-booked.

    How to connect: Settings → Conferencing & Calendars → Add Calendar. Sign in with the calendar account. Approve permissions.

  4. Set your hours

    Tell Cal.com when you're actually available. "Monday–Friday 10 AM to 4 PM" or "Tuesday and Thursday evenings only." Whatever your real life is. Settings → Availability.

  5. Create your first event type

    Click "Event Types" → "+ New". Common ones:

    • Free 15-min intro call — for new leads
    • 30-min consultation — for warm leads, can be paid
    • 60-min session — your actual offering

    For each one: name it, set the length, pick where you meet (Zoom, Google Meet, phone, in-person). Cal.com will auto-generate meeting links.

  6. Take payments if you want (optional)

    In each event's settings, "Apps" → connect Stripe → set a price. Now Cal.com collects payment before they can book.

  7. Add the button to your website

    Two options:

    • Easiest: a link button. Just link your button to https://cal.com/yourusername/event-name. People click → Cal.com opens → they book. Done.
    • Fancier: embedded pop-up. In Cal.com, go to "Event Types" → click "Embed" on the event → copy the HTML snippet → paste into your site (or paste in Claude/Cursor and say "add this to my booking button").

  8. TEST IT.

    Book a test appointment on yourself. Use a different email. Make sure you get the confirmation. Make sure it shows on your real calendar.

Why this is more important than your website's design: A beautiful site with no booking flow loses 80% of leads. An ugly site with a one-click booking button can sustain a 6-figure business. Set this up right.

Module 8

Capture emails (build your list)

Not everyone is ready to book or buy on day one. Most aren't. The single most valuable thing your website can do — besides selling — is collect emails so you can follow up.

An email list is yours forever. Instagram can delete your account tomorrow. Facebook can change its algorithm. Your email list survives all of that. If you do ONE thing besides building the page, build your list.

The trade: they give you their email, you give them something

No one gives their email to "sign up for our newsletter" anymore. You need to offer something specific and valuable. This is called a "lead magnet." Real examples that work:

  • A free guide ("5 Mistakes Most People Make With Their First Tax Filing")
  • A checklist (PDF or notion page)
  • A short video ("3-Minute Hip Mobility Test")
  • A discount code ("$25 off your first session")
  • A free 15-min call (use your Cal.com link)
  • A quiz with personalized results

Connect MailerLite to your form (walkthrough)

  1. In MailerLite, create a "Group"

    A group is just a folder for subscribers. Call it "Website Leads" or similar.

  2. Create your "automation" (the welcome email)

    Automations → New automation → Trigger: when a subscriber joins this group → Action: send an email.

    This email should: (1) deliver the thing you promised, (2) introduce you, (3) tell them what to expect next.

  3. Get your form embed code

    MailerLite → Forms → Embedded forms → New form. Pick the group from step 1. Customize the look. Click "Save and continue" → copy the HTML embed code.

  4. Add the form to your website

    • Carrd: add an "Embed" element, paste the code in.
    • Claude/Cursor: paste the code and say "add this email signup form to my page above the footer."
    • Webflow/Framer: they have native MailerLite integrations. Use those, they look better.

  5. Test it

    Sign up with a different email. Make sure you receive the welcome email. Make sure you can see the subscriber in MailerLite.

Module 9

Design a funnel that actually converts

A funnel is just the journey from "stranger on the internet" to "paying customer." Every site has one, whether you designed it on purpose or not.

Think of it like dating. You don't propose on a first date. Most strangers won't buy from you on the first visit. They need to (1) understand you, (2) trust you, (3) feel safe taking the next step. Your funnel walks them through those stages, on YOUR site, in the right order.

The 5-section homepage that converts

This is the ONLY layout you need for a 1-page conversion site. Sections in order, top to bottom:

  1. HERO (top): the one-liner + ONE button

    The first thing someone sees. Headline: your one-liner from Module 2. Subhead: who it's for + the result. ONE button: your main goal (book a call, get the freebie, buy now).

    Bad: "Welcome to Sarah's Wellness Studio. We offer many services."
    Good: "Burnt-out moms — get strong again in 30 minutes a day, without leaving the house."

  2. PAIN: name their problem

    Show you understand them. 3 sentences max. "Tired of dragging yourself to a gym at 6 AM? Tired of $80 boutique classes? Tired of programs that assume you have 90 minutes and a Pilates reformer?"

    When someone reads this and thinks "she gets me," they keep scrolling.

  3. SOLUTION: what you offer, simply

    3 reasons / 3 features / 3 outcomes. Three is the magic number — easy to scan. Each one gets a small icon, a 2-word title, and one sentence.

  4. PROOF: testimonials, results, credentials

    This is the trust-builder. Real names. Real photos if possible. Real results. If you have no testimonials yet, use credentials, case studies, or "as seen in" logos. If you have none of that, skip this section until you do.

  5. CTA: the same button from the top

    Repeat the button from the hero. Same color. Same words. People who scrolled all the way down are READY. Don't make them scroll back up.

The 3 funnel patterns that actually work

Direct sale (best for low-priced or familiar products)

Website → buy button → checkout → done.

Use this if: you're selling a product under ~$50, OR something people already understand (a printable, a candle, a small service).

Tools: Stripe Payment Links (Module 6).

Free call → sale (best for higher-priced services)

Website → "Book free 15-min call" → Cal.com → call → you close the sale on the call.

Use this if: you're a coach, consultant, contractor, healer, or any service over ~$200.

Tools: Cal.com (Module 7). On the call, have a clear next step ready ("Want to start? I'll send you the booking link right after this.").

Email first → nurture → sale (best for not-ready-yet visitors)

Website → "Get free guide" → email captured → MailerLite sends 5-7 emails over 2 weeks → email #6 makes the offer.

Use this if: your audience is researching, your offer is complex, or you're building a longer-term audience.

Tools: MailerLite (Module 8) + your lead magnet PDF/guide.

Pick ONE funnel pattern for your first site. You can add others later. Beginners ALWAYS try to do all 3 at once. It splits attention and converts no one. Pick one.

The 7 conversion rules (steal these)

  1. One main button per screen. The eye needs to know where to go.
  2. Show your face. A photo of you increases trust 50%+. Doesn't need to be professional. Real beats polished.
  3. Use "you" 3x more than "I" or "we". Read your homepage. Count.
  4. Tell them what happens AFTER they click. "Get instant access" not just "Submit." "Book your call (takes 30 seconds)" not just "Book."
  5. Address the #1 objection right next to the button. If price is the worry: "Free, no credit card needed." If time: "Less than 2 minutes." If commitment: "Cancel anytime."
  6. Keep paragraphs short. 1–3 sentences. Especially on mobile. Walls of text get scrolled past.
  7. Load fast. If your site takes more than 3 seconds, half your traffic leaves before seeing it. Compress images. Don't add 17 fonts.

Module 11

Connect your domain to your site

This is the most "technical" step in the whole guide. It involves something called DNS. We're going to explain it so it makes sense.

DNS = the internet's phone book. When someone types "yoursite.com," DNS looks up what computer (server) actually has the website files. You need to tell DNS: "yoursite.com lives at Vercel/Netlify." That's it. The actual instructions are a one-time copy-paste job.

Connecting to Vercel (works for any domain registrar)

  1. In Vercel, go to your project → Settings → Domains

    Click "Add" and type your domain (e.g., yourbusiness.com). Hit "Add."

  2. Vercel will show you 2–4 DNS records to add

    It looks like:

    Type: A      Name: @       Value: 76.76.21.21
Type: CNAME  Name: www     Value: cname.vercel-dns.com

    Don't panic. You're just copying these values into a different website.

  3. Open your domain registrar in a NEW tab

    (Porkbun, Cloudflare, Namecheap — whichever you bought from.)

  4. Find "DNS" settings for your domain

    • Porkbun: click your domain → "DNS Records" tab
    • Cloudflare: click your domain → "DNS" → "Records"
    • Namecheap: click "Manage" next to your domain → "Advanced DNS" tab

  5. Add the records Vercel gave you

    For each one Vercel listed: click "+ Add record" → pick the Type (A or CNAME) → put the Name → put the Value → Save.

    Critical: if your registrar has any default records pointing somewhere else, DELETE them. You only want the Vercel ones.

  6. Wait 5 minutes to 24 hours

    DNS changes can be instant or can take a day. Most are under 1 hour. Vercel will email you when it's verified. Don't panic if it takes a while.

  7. Test by typing your domain in a browser

    If your site shows up, you're done. If you see a "DNS_PROBE" error, wait longer. If you see someone else's site, you missed deleting an old record.

What about SSL/HTTPS? The little padlock next to your URL? Vercel/Netlify give you that automatically. You don't need to buy an SSL certificate. Anyone trying to sell you one for $50/year is scamming you.

If your builder doesn't use Vercel/Netlify

The process is the same. The host gives you DNS records. You paste them in your registrar. Specifically:

  • Carrd: Pro plan ($19/year) lets you add a custom domain. Settings show you the records to add.
  • Framer: Settings → Domains → Add. Similar pattern.
  • Webflow: Project settings → Hosting → Add Custom Domain. Same pattern.
  • GitHub Pages: Settings → Pages → Custom domain. Add a CNAME file with your domain.
Module 12

Make it mobile-friendly

Over 60% of your visitors will be on a phone. If your site is hard to use on a phone, you lose them. Period.

"Mobile-friendly" doesn't mean "has a mobile version." It means the SAME page reshapes itself to fit any screen — phone, tablet, laptop, desktop. The fancy term for this is "responsive design." Every modern builder and AI tool does this by default. You just need to TEST it.

The 5-minute mobile test

  1. Open your live site on your actual phone

    Not a simulator. Not a "preview" in Chrome. Your real phone. iPhone, Android, doesn't matter — just a real device.

  2. Try every button

    Can you tap them with a thumb without zooming? Are any too small or too close to other buttons?

  3. Read every paragraph

    Is the text legible without zooming? Is it longer than 65 characters per line? (Long lines tire eyes on phones.)

  4. Fill out every form

    Does the keyboard pop up correctly? Does the form actually submit? Did you receive the test email?

  5. Turn the phone sideways

    Does anything break? Does the layout still make sense?

  6. Time how long the page takes to load

    Should be under 3 seconds. If longer: see "speed up" below.

Free mobile testing tools

  • PageSpeed Insights — Google's free tool. Type your URL, get a mobile score 0–100. Anything 70+ is fine. 90+ is great.
  • Responsive Testing Tool — see your site on every screen size at once.
  • Chrome DevTools — right-click → Inspect → click the phone icon at top. Free, built into Chrome.

Speed up a slow site (in priority order)

  1. Compress your images. The #1 reason sites are slow. Use Squoosh (free, by Google) or TinyPNG. Drag your image in, download the smaller version. A 4MB photo can become 200KB with no visible quality loss.
  2. Use the right image format. Photos → JPG (or modern WebP). Logos and graphics with sharp lines → PNG or SVG. Animated → GIF or video.
  3. Don't load 5 different fonts. Pick 2 maximum. One for headlines, one for body text.
  4. Remove unused trackers and analytics. Each one adds load time. You don't need 8 tracking pixels on day 1.
  5. If still slow, ask your AI: "Audit this HTML for performance issues" or "What can I do to make this page load faster on mobile?" with your code pasted in.
Module 13

Get found (Google AND AI search)

A live website nobody finds is a tree falling in an empty forest. There are two main ways people will discover you in 2026: Google search and AI chatbots (ChatGPT, Claude, Perplexity, Gemini). The good news: optimizing for one helps the other.

SEO = Search Engine Optimization (for Google)

AEO = Answer Engine Optimization (for AI)

Google still drives most traffic, but more and more people now ask ChatGPT or Claude for recommendations. To show up in those answers, your site needs to be readable by the AI and say things in a way it can quote you.

The 10 things that actually help (free, beginner-doable)

  1. Write a clear page title and description

    The title tag is what shows up in Google's blue link. Make it specific to YOU and what you do. Bad: "Home." Good: "Erwin TN Bookkeeper for Small Businesses — Free Consultation"

  2. Use real headings (H1, H2, H3)

    Don't just make text bigger. Use heading tags. Both Google and AI use them to understand structure. One H1 per page. Multiple H2s for sections. H3s for sub-sections.

  3. Answer the questions your customers actually ask

    Add a FAQ section with real questions in plain language. ("How much does it cost?" "Do you work with people outside Tennessee?" "What if I don't have a business yet?") AI assistants pull answers directly from FAQ sections.

  4. Include your location if it matters

    Service businesses: put your city/region in the H1, the title, and a few places on the page. "Asheville therapist" beats "Therapist" for local searches.

  5. Get backlinks (other sites linking to yours)

    The single biggest Google ranking factor. Easy ways: get listed on Google Business Profile (free, mandatory for local), get on local directories, ask people you've helped to mention you, write a guest post somewhere.

  6. Create a Google Business Profile

    google.com/business — free. This is what creates that map listing on the right of Google when someone searches you. If you serve local customers, this is more important than your website.

  7. Add structured data (schema)

    This is invisible code that tells Google "this is a business, this is a price, this is a review." Ask Claude: "Add Local Business schema to my homepage with [your details]." Paste the result. Done in 30 seconds.

  8. Write content that AI can quote

    AI assistants like clear, definitive statements. "The average tax bookkeeping for a freelancer costs $300–$600 per year" is quotable. "It depends on a lot of factors" is not.

  9. Submit your sitemap to Google

    Once your domain is live, go to Google Search Console → add your site → submit your sitemap (Vercel/Netlify usually generate one at /sitemap.xml automatically).

  10. Make sure your site is crawlable by AI

    By default it usually is. Don't add a "robots.txt" that blocks AI crawlers like GPTBot, Claude-Web, or PerplexityBot unless you want to be invisible to them.

The honest truth about SEO: ranking on Google takes months. Sometimes a year. Don't obsess over it on day one. Focus on getting your first 10 customers through any means (social, word of mouth, in person). Set up the basics above and let SEO build over time.

What NOT to waste money on (yet)

  • SEO agencies charging $1,000+/month — completely unnecessary at your stage
  • "SEO tools" like Ahrefs, SEMrush — overkill for a brand new site
  • Paid backlinks — Google will penalize you
  • "Submit your site to 500 directories" services — 99% are spam
  • Most paid keyword research tools — free Google Search Console gives you 90% of what you need

Git + GitHub Desktop (so your code lives on GitHub)

"Git" is the version-control system. "GitHub" is the website that hosts your Git stuff. To push your local code to GitHub, the easiest way is GitHub Desktop (no terminal needed).

  1. Download GitHub Desktop

    Go to desktop.github.com → download → install.

  2. Sign in with your GitHub account

    The same one from Module 4.

  3. Click "Add an Existing Repository" → pick your project folder

    If it says "this isn't a Git repository" → "create a repository here" → fill in the name and click Create.

Reference

Glossary — every techy word, explained

If something on this page sounds like a foreign language, find it here.

Frontend

The part of a website you can SEE. The pictures, words, buttons, colors. What the visitor experiences.

Backend

The invisible machinery. Where form submissions go. Where databases live. The "behind the scenes" of a site.

HTML

The skeleton of a webpage. Headings, paragraphs, buttons — all written as HTML tags like <h1> or <button>.

CSS

The styling. Colors, fonts, sizes, layout. CSS dresses HTML up.

JavaScript (JS)

What makes a page DO things. Click a button → something happens. Form validation. Animations. Anything interactive.

Hosting

The computer your website lives on, that serves your page to anyone who visits. Vercel, Netlify, Cloudflare Pages = hosting services.

Domain

The address you type to find a website. yourbusiness.com. You "rent" it for about $10/year from a registrar (Porkbun, Cloudflare).

DNS

"Domain Name System." The internet's phone book. Tells computers where a domain actually lives.

SSL / HTTPS

The little padlock next to a URL. Means the connection is encrypted (safe). Free and automatic from any modern host.

Git

A system for saving every version of your code. Like time travel for files. You "commit" changes to save snapshots.

GitHub

A website that hosts your Git stuff in the cloud. Free. Owned by Microsoft.

Repository (Repo)

A single project folder on GitHub. Has all your code + every version of it.

Deploy

The act of pushing your code so it shows up on the live internet. "I deployed the new version" = "the live site is updated."

Terminal / Command Line

The black-screen-with-text app. Where you type commands instead of clicking. Looks scary, isn't.

API

"Application Programming Interface." A way two pieces of software talk to each other. "Use Claude's API" = "let your app send messages to Claude and get answers back."

CMS

"Content Management System." Lets you edit your site's content without coding. WordPress, Webflow's editor, etc.

Framework

A pre-built foundation of code. React, Next.js, Vue. You don't NEED one to build a website. Single HTML files work fine.

SEO

"Search Engine Optimization." Doing things to your site so Google ranks it higher.

AEO

"Answer Engine Optimization." Making sure AI chatbots (ChatGPT, Claude, Perplexity) cite YOU when answering questions.

Lead Magnet

A free thing you give in exchange for an email. A guide, a checklist, a quiz, a discount code.

Funnel

The journey from "stranger sees your site" to "stranger becomes a customer." Has a beginning, middle, and end.

CTA

"Call to Action." The button or text that asks the visitor to do something. "Book now," "Get the guide," "Buy."

Responsive Design

A site that reshapes for any screen size — phone, tablet, laptop, desktop. The same page, smart layout.

Schema / Structured Data

Invisible code that tells search engines what a page is about. "This is a business. This is its address. This is a review."

Subdomain

A piece in front of the main domain. blog.yourbusiness.com is a subdomain of yourbusiness.com. Free to create.

Server

A computer somewhere in the world that runs 24/7 and serves your website to visitors. You're renting one when you use Vercel/Netlify.

Static Site

A site where the pages are pre-built and don't change for each visitor. Fast, cheap to host. What most small business sites should be.

Dynamic Site

A site where pages change depending on the user, time, database content. Slower, more expensive. Needed for apps with logins.

When things break

If you get stuck

The 4-step unstucking method (in order)

  1. Read the actual error message

    Computers tell you what's wrong if you read what's on the screen. Most beginners panic and don't read. Read it. Copy the exact error text.

  2. Paste the error into Claude/ChatGPT

    "I'm getting this error: [paste]. I'm trying to [what you're doing]. Here's my code: [paste]. Help me debug."

    Be specific. The more context, the better the answer.

  3. Google the exact error

    Someone else has had this exact problem. Stack Overflow, Reddit, GitHub issues — paste it in. Take the top 2–3 results, read them.

  4. Try one fix at a time

    Don't change 5 things at once. Change one thing, test, undo if it didn't work. Repeat. This is how every developer alive does this.
